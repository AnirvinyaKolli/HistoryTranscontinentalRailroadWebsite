Construction

The construction of the Transcontinental Railroad was an engineering challenge that required careful planning and a large labor force.

Labor

The workforce is divided into two major companies:

  • Union Pacific relied heavily on Irish immigrants, Civil War veterans, and formerly enslaved African Americans.
  • The Central Pacific depended on Chinese immigrant labor, eventually hiring 15,000-20,000 Chinese workers because white laborers were scarce and unwilling to perform the dangerous work.

Chinese workers were paid less than white workers (around $30-35/month, compared to $40-50/month for white workers) and had to pay for their own food and lodging.

Laborers endured brutal conditions:

  • Extreme heat in the deserts of Nevada and Utah.
  • Deadly winter storms in the Sierra Nevada.
  • Frequent accidents from dynamite blasting, rockslides, and collapsing tunnels.

Despite discrimination, Chinese workers became known for their discipline, efficiency, and ability to complete dangerous tasks such as tunneling through granite using nitroglycerin.

Workers often lived in temporary camps with poor sanitation, limited medical care, and long workdays (10-12 hours).

Additional facts: The total workforce peaked at around 40,000 workers. Estimates suggest 1,200-1,500 deaths occurred during construction, with Chinese workers suffering disproportionately higher casualty rates due to the most hazardous assignments.

The Central Pacific employed approximately 10,000 to 15,000 Chinese laborers at its peak, who made up about 80-90% of its workforce. They were recruited primarily from Guangdong province in China, fleeing poverty, famine, and conflicts like the Taiping Rebellion. Pay disparities were significant: Chinese workers initially earned $31 per month (equivalent to about $810 in 2025 dollars), while white workers received $30 per month but often held supervisory roles. After a successful strike in 1867, Chinese wages were raised to $35 per month. Living conditions were harsh; workers lived in crowded camps with minimal amenities, facing extreme weather, disease outbreaks like cholera, and isolation. Death tolls were high, with estimates of 600-1,000 Chinese workers dying from accidents, avalanches, explosions, and illnesses. Despite these challenges, their contributions were pivotal, as they built most of the western track, including 15 tunnels through the Sierra Nevada, using manual labor and explosives like nitroglycerin.

  • Union Pacific workforce included about 8,000-10,000 workers, mostly Irish immigrants and Civil War veterans, who faced similar dangers but had slightly better pay and conditions.
  • Contributions extended beyond labor; Chinese workers innovated techniques for blasting and tunneling, proving essential to the project's success.

Planning

Engineers had to survey terrain to determine efficient routes. The project required coordination across long distances. Rail lines were built simultaneously from east and west, meeting at Promontory Summit in 1869.

Planning began with the Pacific Railroad Surveys conducted from 1853 to 1855 under the Department of War, involving expeditions that mapped over 400,000 square miles of the West, documenting natural history, geology, and potential routes. Political debates raged over the route: northern routes favored by Asa Whitney and others to connect Chicago and the Great Lakes, versus central routes through the Sierra Nevada and Great Basin. The Pacific Railroad Acts of 1862 and 1864 authorized construction, providing federal land grants totaling about 130 million acres (larger than Texas) and bonds worth $55 million at 6% interest. Coordination involved the federal government overseeing two companies—Central Pacific from California and Union Pacific from Nebraska—building westward and eastward simultaneously. Costs escalated to around $50-60 million total, with land grants valued at billions in today's dollars. Timelines were ambitious: authorized in 1862, ground broken in 1863, but delays from the Civil War pushed completion to 1869, just six years later.

  • Surveys identified multiple routes, with the chosen path crossing the Continental Divide and Sierra Nevada.
  • Coordination challenges included supply chains, as materials were shipped around South America or via Panama, costing twice as much for the latter.
  • Political opposition from Southern states delayed funding until after secession.

Engineering Feats

  • 15 tunnels were blasted through the Sierra Nevada Mountains.
  • Numerous bridges were constructed to cross rivers and valleys.
  • Harsh environments, including mountains, deserts, and extreme weather, had to be overcome.

Issues

Corruption & Fraud

Financial corruption plagued the project, exemplified by the Crédit Mobilier scandal. The construction company, Crédit Mobilier of America, was owned by Union Pacific executives and charged inflated prices for work, leading to overpayments and profits funneled to politicians. Congressman Oakes Ames was implicated, distributing shares to influence votes. The scandal erupted in 1872, contributing to public distrust and the downfall of several officials.

  • Union Pacific paid Crédit Mobilier about $23 million more than actual costs.
  • This led to investigations and reforms in railroad financing.

Violence & Conflict

Violence arose from conflicts with Native American tribes, who saw the railroad as a threat to their lands and buffalo herds. Attacks on survey parties and workers led to U.S. Army protection, including cavalry patrols and buffalo hunts to deprive tribes of food. Notable incidents included raids by Sioux and Cheyenne, resulting in deaths on both sides. The railroad also displaced tribes, exacerbating tensions.

  • By the late 19th century, buffalo populations dropped to near extinction, affecting Plains tribes.
  • General William Tecumseh Sherman prioritized railroad protection, stating "the only good Indian is a dead Indian."

Environmental Challenges

The terrain posed immense obstacles, including the Sierra Nevada mountains, deserts, and extreme weather. Workers battled avalanches, floods, and heat, with snow galleries built to keep tracks open in winter. Environmental impact included deforestation for ties and fuel, and disruption of wildlife habitats.

  • 37 miles of snow sheds were constructed in the Sierras to prevent avalanches.
  • Rivers like the Platte and Green were bridged, but floods caused delays.

Worker Safety

Dangerous conditions led to numerous accidents, including explosions from nitroglycerin, tunnel collapses, and falls. Poor medical care and long hours exacerbated risks, with estimates of 1,200-2,000 total deaths across the project.

  • Chinese workers faced higher risks in tunneling, with many fatalities from blasts.
  • Union Pacific workers dealt with attacks and harsh plains weather.

Financial Problems

Costs overran budgets due to unforeseen difficulties like tunneling and weather. Bond issues and land sales failed to cover expenses initially, leading to reliance on government subsidies. The Panic of 1873 worsened financial woes.

  • Total cost was about $50 million, but land grants provided long-term value.
  • Companies issued their own bonds, but defaults were common until profitability.

Despite these challenges, the railroad was completed in just six years; a major achievement in American engineering.
